Bolt Torque Reference Notes

  • All values assume dry, clean, unlubricated threads (friction coefficient K ≈ 0.20). Reduce by ~25–30% for lubricated threads.
  • Metric grades: first number × 100 = min tensile strength (MPa). Product of both numbers × 10 = min yield strength (MPa). E.g. 8.8 → 800 MPa tensile, 640 MPa yield.
  • ASTM A325 and A490 values are for snug-tight minimum; structural connections may require turn-of-nut or direct tension method per AISC/RCSC specifications.
  • Values represent typical recommendations. Always confirm with project structural or electrical engineer of record.

Electrical Termination Notes

  • Anti-oxidant compound: Required for all aluminum conductor terminations. Apply to conductor and contact surfaces before installation.
  • Two-hole lugs: Always torque both bolts in the sequence specified by the OEM (typically inner first, then outer).
  • Re-torque intervals: Inspect and re-torque electrical connections after initial energization and at 1-year intervals for aluminum conductors.
  • NEC 110.14: Conductor and terminal materials must be compatible (Cu–Cu, Al–Al, or rated Cu–Al). Check lug markings (AL9CU, AL7CU, CU ONLY, etc.).

Engineering Notes

  • Lubrication matters: Dry vs. lubricated threads can change required torque by 30–40%. Always confirm if the spec assumes dry (Kdry ≈ 0.2) or lubricated (Klube ≈ 0.15) threads.
  • Copper vs. aluminum lugs: Aluminum conductors require anti-oxidant compound and lower re-torque intervals due to cold flow/creep. Always check OEM guidance.
  • Belleville washers: Many inverter and transformer busbar connections specify Belleville (spring) washers to maintain clamp force during thermal cycling.
  • Calibrated torque wrenches: Use only calibrated tools. Verify calibration before each day of torquing activity.
